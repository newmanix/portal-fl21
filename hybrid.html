Hybrid workplace model

Introduction

A hybrid workplace model is a model that mixes on-premise and remote workplace models to offer a more flexible
environment to employees. It can be almost entirely remote, with employees attending regular face-to-face
meetings. It can also be where employees alternate time in the office with some remote work. A hybrid
workplace provides the employees with a better work-life balance that has more engagement. Moreover,
employers benefit from a hybrid workplace by building a more productive and stable workforce.



The pros of hybrid jobs

  1. Lower operational costs

    2. In a hybrid work model, companies will experience a decrease in utility bills as they will not need as many
    computers, printers, desks, and other office supplies. Especially, in expensive metro areas, companies can save
    a significant amount of money on real estate expenses by implementing this work model. The hybrid work model will
    benefit the employees as well by creating an opportunity for them to spend less on daily commutes and other
    workplace-related expenses.

  2. Increased productivity

    3. A hybrid model boosts productivity by offering flexibility in a way that empowers employees to work to their
    strengths. In a hybrid work model, companies can encourage a culture that views the remote component of this
    work model as a positive option for completing deep-focus tasks. This working model is important to increase
    productivity by creating the opportunity for employees to have their own personal space and time to focus on
    their respective tasks. Additionally, there will be meetings in the office with teams for a good balance of
    creativity and collaboration.

The cons of hybrid jobs

  1. Difficulty keeping up with hybrid schedules

    2. More variation in the schedules of a team can be mentioned as one of the challenges of the hybrid workplace
    model; the flexibility that the model provides for each team member can lead to schedule conflict when
    arranging team meetings. And, it can also be tough for employees that come on-site to know which of their
    teammates will be available unless efficient tools are implemented. This can potentially lead to resourcing
    issues. The use of different work plans such as cohort schedules or staggered schedules along with the
    creation of policies and guidelines around employee schedules will help avoid any frustration or confusion by
    promoting a smooth, collaborative and creative work environment.

  2. Faster employee burnout

    3. In the remote component of this workplace model, employees may tend to feel guilty for taking a time off in
    the afternoon which they overcompensate by working later. If the remote team is left unchecked, they could be
    working longer hours and taking fewer breaks than the on-site teams in which case, the culture of burnout
    might occur. Creating a culture where employees can take breaks when they are feeling overwhelmed will help
    fight remote burnout. Also, setting up an office that is efficient, productive, and delightful will help
    reduce on-site burnout.
